Montebello, Québec · Day Trip

Parc Oméga

A wildlife safari you drive through, paced for a relaxed day with your grandfather

Parc Oméga is a rare day trip where most of the experience happens from the comfort of your car. You drive a slow loop through open habitat while elk, deer, bison, and wolves roam freely around you, and a few come right up to the window. For an older visitor it is close to ideal: hours of close wildlife with very little walking, plus two short, accessible trails to stretch your legs whenever you feel like it.

About 120 km each way, roughly a 1 hour 25 minute drive via Hwy 417 east through Ottawa, then Autoroute 50 E across Gatineau and Route 323 N into Montebello. On Saturdays this season the park opens at 9:00 AM and closes at 6:00 PM, with last entry at 4:00 PM. Leaving at 9:00 AM still gets you there before the park's busiest stretch, with enough room for the safari drive, a proper lunch break, and the 2:00 PM wolf-feeding talk.

01

The safari drive

Deer and elk at the window

The heart of the visit is a roughly 12 to 15 km loop you drive at a crawl. Deer and elk approach the car for a carrot, often leaning right in. Keep carrots ready in a bag cut into pieces and let him hand them out from the passenger seat.

Bison, boar, and grazing herds

Further along, the open range holds bison, wild boar, and caribou. These are watch-from-the-car zones, so it is all viewing with no effort. Roll the windows down slowly for photos.

Wolves and bears

The drive passes wolf and black bear habitats, viewed safely from inside. There is also a larger wolf observatory later in the walking zone if you want a closer, unhurried look.

02

Two short walks, when you feel like it

Land of the Pioneers

A flat, stroller and walker friendly area with a 19th-century settlement, a sugar shack, and a wolf observatory. A park ranger hosts wolf-feeding talks at noon, 2:00, and 4:00, which are worth timing your stop around. La Cookerie food shack is here too.

First Nations Trail

A gentle one-kilometre loop past totem poles and a small lake, with displays on the cultures of eleven First Nations communities. Deer often wander the path here too, so keep a few carrots in your pocket.

03

Food, carrots, and the Park House

Lunch, or a packed picnic

On-site food is limited to counter fare like poutine and hot dogs at La Cookerie, plus the Omegabon café at the entrance. Given that, packing your own picnic is the easiest call. There are picnic areas in the park, and it lets you eat exactly what you want on your own schedule.

Carrots and the gift shop

Buy carrots at the Park House carrot cart on the way in, or bring your own from home to save money. The main gift shop and an arctic wolf observatory are here too. Note that carrots are the only food allowed for the animals.

Practical tips

Stay in the car on the trailFor safety, you cannot get out anywhere on the driving loop, even for photos. Keep all doors closed and only lower windows partway when animals approach.
Carrots only, and bring plentyCarrots are the only food allowed for the animals. Bringing your own from home is cheaper than the carrot cart; a large bag goes quickly when the deer line up.
Easy on mobilityThe hard work is all done by the car. The two walking zones are flat and accessible for walkers and wheelchairs, so do as much or as little on foot as suits him.
Dress for the weather and bring cashYou will be in and out of the car, so layers help. Bring a little cash for the sugar shack maple products, which are not always on card.

A calm, close-up encounter with Canadian wildlife that asks almost nothing of his legs. The kind of outing that delivers a lot of wonder for very little effort, and gives the two of you plenty to talk about on the drive home.
